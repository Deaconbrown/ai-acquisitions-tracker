This week's biggest deal
SpaceX acquires xAI in landmark consolidation of Musk's AI empire
Reported by NBC News →

SpaceX absorbing xAI is not a conventional acquisition. It is a structural bet that the future of AI requires more than model capability. It requires the compute, launch infrastructure, and satellite connectivity that only SpaceX can provide at scale. Elon Musk has spent years building parallel companies that each control a critical layer of the technology stack. This move snaps those layers together. xAI gets rocket-grade infrastructure. SpaceX gets a proprietary AI brain. The combined entity becomes a vertically integrated force with no obvious equivalent anywhere in the industry.

For investors, the signal here is about ownership architecture. Musk is not licensing AI capability or forming a partnership. He is consolidating control inside a single corporate structure, one that remains private and outside the reach of public market scrutiny. That limits transparency but accelerates decision-making. It also raises serious questions for regulators who are already watching the concentration of AI power among a handful of individuals. This deal will draw more scrutiny than any term sheet this year, and rightly so. The strategic logic is sound. The governance questions are not going away.

What to watch
This week's deals reveal two forces running in parallel. At the top of the market, Musk is collapsing company boundaries to build a vertically integrated AI empire, while Meta is spending aggressively on talent, paying more than two billion dollars for a single startup to staff its Superintelligence Labs. Below that tier, the pattern is domain specialisation. Microsoft targets financial research with Fintool. Thomson Reuters locks in legal deal data with Noetica. Communify and Empaxis combine in wealth management. Figma buys Weavy to hold ground in AI-assisted design. The market is bifurcating between empire builders and vertical specialists, and the gap between those two strategies is widening fast. Watch whether the $60 billion SpaceX bid for Cursor, the AI coding tool, moves from rumour to term sheet. If it closes, it would be the largest AI acquisition on record and signal that infrastructure players are now willing to pay software company multiples for tools that sit inside the developer workflow.
