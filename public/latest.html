This week's biggest deal
Beijing blocks Meta's acquisition of AI start-up Manus, amid US-China rivalry
Reported by Taipei Times →

Beijing's decision to block Meta's bid for Manus, the Chinese AI agent startup, is the clearest signal yet that the Chinese state views frontier AI talent and technology as a strategic asset too valuable to export to American firms. The WSJ reported separately this week that leaders of another Chinese AI firm acquired by Meta have been restricted from leaving the country, suggesting this is coordinated policy rather than a one-off ruling. For acquirers, this rewrites the due diligence playbook: a signed deal with a China-based AI team is no longer a closed deal until Beijing says so. The pattern confirms that cross-border AI acquisitions involving Chinese targets now carry sovereign risk that no amount of legal structuring can fully neutralise.

What to watch
Two themes dominate this week and they pull in opposite directions. On one side, acquirers in the West are snapping up AI security and observability capabilities at speed: Akamai paying $205m for LayerX and Snowflake closing its Observe deal both reflect the same logic, that enterprises will pay a premium to make AI deployments auditable and defensible. On the other side, Beijing is making clear that Chinese AI assets are not freely transferable to American buyers. The Manus block and the travel restrictions on personnel tied to Meta's earlier deal are the opening moves in what looks like a systematic policy of AI talent retention by the Chinese state. Acquirers with existing or prospective deals involving China-based teams should assume regulatory clearance is now the floor, not the ceiling, of sovereign risk. Watch for similar exit restrictions to be tested in South Korea and other jurisdictions where state interest in AI is rising.
