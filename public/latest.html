This week's biggest deal
Leaders of AI Firm Bought by Meta Are Restricted From Leaving China
Reported by WSJ →

The Wall Street Journal's report that Chinese authorities are blocking the founders of a Meta-acquired AI firm from leaving China turns a routine acqui-hire into a geopolitical standoff. Meta paid for the talent and the technology, but Beijing now controls whether that talent actually transfers, exposing a critical flaw in the cross-border acquisition playbook. For any Western company looking to absorb Chinese AI teams, this episode makes clear that exit restrictions are a deal risk as real as valuation or IP ownership. The broader signal is stark: acquiring AI capability across the US-China divide is no longer simply a matter of corporate negotiation.

What to watch
Three patterns cut across this week's deals. First, geopolitical friction is now a first-order deal risk: the Meta-China standoff and Scale AI's ICG acquisition both show that national security considerations are reshaping who can buy what and whether acquired talent can actually move. Second, infrastructure spending is accelerating ahead of AI demand rather than in response to it, as MARA's $1.5 billion energy pivot makes plain. Third, legacy platform companies such as Akamai and Phenom are using acquisitions to bolt on AI capability at the layer closest to their existing customers, browser security and HR software respectively, rather than building from scratch. Watch for more of these tuck-in deals at the application layer in the weeks ahead, and watch whether Beijing moves to restrict further talent transfers tied to US acquisitions of China-based AI teams.
