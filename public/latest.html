This week's biggest deal
Meta pushes deeper into AI robots with acquisition
Reported by Sherwood News →

Meta's move into AI robotics through acquisition marks a deliberate expansion beyond software and large language models into physical intelligence, a domain where Google, Amazon, and a clutch of well-funded startups are already competing hard. The robotics bet is consistent with Meta's broader push to own the full stack of AI capability rather than depend on third parties for hardware or embodied systems. At the same time, the week also brought news that Meta is walking away from its planned acquisition of Chinese AI agent firm Manus after Beijing blocked the deal, a reminder that geopolitical friction is now a live variable in every cross-border AI transaction. Together these two Meta stories define the twin pressures shaping big-tech AI strategy right now: aggressive expansion in some directions, forced retreat in others.

What to watch
This week's deals reveal three converging forces in AI acquisition activity. First, vertical specialisation is accelerating: buyers in clinical trials, power markets, and finance are snapping up narrow AI tools rather than waiting to build them, which means domain-specific AI startups now carry a clearer path to exit. Second, the hardware and infrastructure layer is heating up, with Applied Materials buying into advanced chip packaging and NEC closing its CSG deal, signalling that acquirers see the physical stack as just as contested as the software layer. Third, and most consequentially, the collapse of Meta's Manus deal shows that Chinese regulatory approval is now a deal variable that boards and investors must price in from the term sheet stage. Watch for more buyers to quietly add geopolitical due diligence clauses, and watch whether Meta's robotics acquisition signals a broader pivot by large-cap tech firms toward embodied AI as the next arena for capability accumulation.
