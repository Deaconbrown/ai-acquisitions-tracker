This week's biggest deal
Beijing Blocks Meta's Acquisition of AI Start-up Manus, Amid US-China Rivalry
Reported by Taipei Times →

Beijing's decision to block Meta's bid for Manus is the clearest signal yet that AI acquisitions are now a front line in the US-China technology rivalry. China is using exit controls not just as a diplomatic tool but as a mechanism to keep frontier AI talent and IP inside its borders, as the separate WSJ report on restricted Manus leaders confirms. For Meta, the failure means its agentic AI roadmap hits a structural wall it cannot buy its way through. For every acquirer eyeing a Chinese-founded AI team, this week draws a hard line: geopolitics now sits at the top of the deal risk checklist.

What to watch
Two patterns define this week and will shape the weeks ahead. First, state power is now an active variable in AI M&A. Beijing's moves against the Manus deal and its Manus founders show that governments will use exit controls, not just antitrust, to shape who captures AI value. Any deal touching China-based teams or IP needs political risk analysis on par with financial due diligence. Second, the talent bleed at xAI after the SpaceX merger is a warning about integration risk in founder-led AI companies. Researchers who joined for a specific mission do not automatically transfer their loyalty to a new corporate parent. Watch for more consolidation across the browser and observability layers, where Atlassian, Akamai, and Snowflake all moved this week, as large platforms race to own the interface between knowledge workers and AI systems.
